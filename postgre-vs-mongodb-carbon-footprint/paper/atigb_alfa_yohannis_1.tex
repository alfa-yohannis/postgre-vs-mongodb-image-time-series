\title{Carbon Footprint of Document-oriented and Relational Storage for Image-based Time-Series Workloads}

\author{
[Hidden for double-blind review]

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Smart building and facility-monitoring systems increasingly deploy IoT cameras that store high-resolution images inline in time-series databases. Selecting the right storage backend affects not only performance but also energy consumption and carbon emissions---a growing concern for sustainable building operations. This paper measures and compares the carbon footprint of PostgreSQL (TimescaleDB) and MongoDB for image-based time-series workloads across four resolutions ranging from 1080p to 5K. Using isolated Dockerized deployments and hardware-level energy tracking via CodeCarbon, we find that the preferred engine depends on the target resolution. At lower resolutions, MongoDB completes workloads faster and therefore emits less carbon despite comparable per-second power consumption. Above 1440p, however, PostgreSQL becomes both faster and more energy-efficient, as MongoDB's inline document storage incurs increasing write amplification and cache pressure that prolong execution and raise energy use. PostgreSQL's TOAST mechanism, which relocates large binary payloads out-of-line, maintains stable storage amplification across all resolutions and yields a carbon advantage at 4K and above under the conditions tested. These findings provide resolution-dependent guidance for carbon-aware database selection in green IoT deployments.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
time-series databases, IoT building monitoring, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, carbon footprint, green computing, inline image storage

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern smart building and facility-monitoring systems deploy dense networks of IoT cameras and environmental sensors to support occupancy analysis, safety auditing, and predictive maintenance~\cite{bajera_iot_smart_buildings,alfuqaha_iot_survey}. These devices continuously capture high-resolution images---from 1080p security feeds to 4K inspection cameras---which must be stored inline alongside timestamped metadata for temporal queries and audit replay~\cite{ceec_tsdb_review,jensen_tsms_survey}. As green building standards increasingly account for the operational carbon cost of IT infrastructure~\cite{nerc_tsdb_survey}, the choice of storage backend is no longer purely a performance decision: it directly affects the energy consumed by the data tier running 24/7 at the edge or in an on-premises data center.

Organizations commonly reuse existing general-purpose databases for this role. PostgreSQL~\cite{postgresql_official} with TimescaleDB~\cite{timescaledb_official} extends a proven relational engine with hypertable chunking and employs TOAST~\cite{postgresql_toast_docs} to relocate large binary payloads out-of-line. MongoDB~\cite{mongodb_official} offers native Time-Series Collections backed by the WiredTiger~\cite{mongodb_wiredtiger_storage} storage engine with Snappy~\cite{google_snappy} block compression, storing entire BSON documents---including multi-megabyte image binaries---inline in B-Tree pages~\cite{timescale_mongodb_comparison}. This inline vs.\ out-of-line architectural difference is expected to produce divergent energy profiles as image resolution grows.

Existing benchmarks~\cite{tsbs,cratedb_ts_benchmark,diva_timescaledb_mongodb_eval} target lightweight numeric payloads and do not measure energy or emissions. No study evaluates the carbon cost of storing multi-megabyte image frames across varying resolutions in containerized environments. Therefore, this work aims to answer this research question: \textit{What is the carbon footprint of inline image-based time-series storage in PostgreSQL (TimescaleDB) versus MongoDB across varying camera resolutions, and which engine is more energy-efficient for IoT building monitoring?}

We benchmark both engines across four resolutions (1080p--5K) using Docker containers, measure end-to-end CO$_2$ emissions with CodeCarbon~\cite{codecarbon}, and report supporting performance metrics to explain the observed energy differences.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Time-series databases optimize sequential data ingestion by employing append-only storage, time-based partitioning, and columnar compression to bypass the write amplification inherent in traditional B-Tree indexes~\cite{dewaal_timeseries_survey,jensen_tsms_survey}. TimescaleDB~\cite{timescaledb_official} extends PostgreSQL~\cite{postgresql_official} with transparent hypertable chunking that sustains flat ingestion throughput at scale~\cite{dewaal_timeseries_survey,nerc_tsdb_survey}. MongoDB~\cite{mongodb_official} introduced native Time-Series Collections in version 5.0, which internally bucket measurements into compacted documents optimized for columnar access patterns~\cite{timescale_mongodb_bad_idea,mongodb_ts_best_practices}.

Existing comparative benchmarks between these systems are limited to lightweight scalar payloads. The TSBS suite~\cite{tsbs} generates synthetic JSON/SQL events of a few bytes each. The evaluation by Mohamud and Broström~\cite{diva_timescaledb_mongodb_eval} compared TimescaleDB and MongoDB query execution times using numerical sensor data. CrateDB's benchmark~\cite{cratedb_ts_benchmark} focused on aggregate throughput over numeric IoT metrics. These studies typically do not incorporate binary payloads exceeding a few kilobytes.

This gap is significant because storage engine behavior changes fundamentally with payload size. MongoDB's WiredTiger~\cite{mongodb_wiredtiger_storage} uses Snappy~\cite{google_snappy} block compression that achieves excellent ratios on repetitive JSON structures but offers negligible compression on high-entropy binary data such as JPEG images. PostgreSQL's TOAST~\cite{postgresql_toast_docs} mechanism stores oversized attributes out-of-line in a separate table, an approach specifically designed for large binary values. MongoDB's alternative for large binaries---GridFS~\cite{mongodb_gridfs}---chunks files into 255~KB segments, but is incompatible with native Time-Series Collections~\cite{mongodb_ts_limitations}.

The carbon footprint of computing infrastructure has attracted growing attention. Lannelongue et al.~\cite{lannelongue_green_algorithms} introduced the Green Algorithms framework, quantifying CO$_2$-equivalent emissions from computation using energy consumption and regional grid intensity---the same principle adopted by CodeCarbon~\cite{codecarbon}. Strubell et al.~\cite{strubell_energy_nlp} highlighted the disproportionate energy cost of large-scale model training, catalysing interest in per-operation energy accounting across the software stack. Within the database domain, energy efficiency studies have focused on query optimisation~\cite{nerc_tsdb_survey} and hardware selection, but no prior work applies per-operation carbon accounting to binary time-series storage engines under varying payload sizes.

Our work directly addresses this gap by conducting a systematic multi-resolution benchmark (1080p--5K) for IoT building monitoring, measuring both performance and carbon footprint via CodeCarbon.


\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Hardware and Software Environment}
All experiments execute on a single host machine running Linux 6.8.0-107 with an 11th Gen Intel Core i9-11900H processor (8 cores / 16 threads, 2.50~GHz base) and 31~GB RAM. The machine is located in Indonesia (Banten grid region); CodeCarbon uses the corresponding national carbon intensity factor. CPU and RAM power are measured via Intel RAPL hardware counters (\texttt{tracking\_mode=machine}). Containers share host CPU and memory with no explicit CPU pinning or memory limits beyond the MongoDB WiredTiger cache cap. Table~\ref{tab:software_versions} lists the software stack.

\subsection{Environment and Workloads}
All experiments run in isolated Docker~\cite{docker_official} containers: PostgreSQL~15 with TimescaleDB~\cite{timescaledb_official} using default shared buffers, and MongoDB~7.0~\cite{mongodb_official} with \texttt{--wiredTigerCacheSizeGB~30}, allocating nearly all available system RAM (31~GB) to the WiredTiger buffer pool. This server-grade configuration allows the complete working set to reside in memory at lower resolutions, isolating the effect of inline document size from cache-pressure artefacts at modest scales. Four quality-90 JPEG resolutions simulate building-camera payloads: 1080p ($1920{\times}1080$, $\sim$0.80~MB), 1440p ($2560{\times}1440$, $\sim$1.40~MB), 4K~UHD ($3840{\times}2160$, $\sim$2.99~MB), and 5K ($5120{\times}2880$, $\sim$5.09~MB). High-quality JPEG at 90 retains high visual entropy that largely defeats internal block compression, reflecting realistic inline storage conditions.

\subsection{Schema and Operations}
PostgreSQL stores each frame as a \texttt{BYTEA} row in a TimescaleDB hypertable; TOAST~\cite{postgresql_toast_docs} automatically relocates payloads exceeding $\sim$2~KB out-of-line. MongoDB stores functionally equivalent documents with the binary payload as inline \texttt{BinData}~\cite{mongodb_bson_spec} in a native Time-Series Collection (Snappy-compressed WiredTiger B-Tree). Both schemas carry a compound index on \texttt{(device\_id, ts~DESC)}.

For each resolution, 2,000 records are inserted in batches of 50 across 5 runs, followed by 5 binary retrieval runs that fully materialise the \texttt{payload\_data} column for 2,000 rows. Each engine is wiped and rebuilt between resolutions. Storage amplification is measured as on-disk size divided by raw payload size.

\subsection{Carbon Footprint Measurement}
Each engine phase runs as a single CodeCarbon~\cite{codecarbon}-tracked process. CodeCarbon reads CPU and RAM power via Intel RAPL hardware counters and converts total energy to CO$_2$-equivalent using the regional grid intensity for Indonesia ($\approx$0.676~kg CO$_2$eq/kWh). All operations for a given engine (insertions and retrievals across all four resolutions) are aggregated into one measurement, isolating per-engine carbon cost under identical hardware conditions. Per-resolution emissions are estimated using a constant power-rate model: the measured total energy $E$ and duration $T$ yield an average power $P = E/T$; each profile's estimated energy is then $P \times t_i$, where $t_i$ is the sum of mean insertion and retrieval times for that profile across all runs. This model assumes constant CPU and RAM power draw throughout the phase, providing a principled first-order breakdown consistent with CodeCarbon's measurement approach.

\subsection{Supporting Performance Observations}
\label{subsec:performance}
Table~\ref{tab:performance_summary} and Figures~\ref{fig:insert_throughput}--\ref{fig:retrieval_latency} summarise the performance results. Insert throughput crosses over between 1440p and 4K: MongoDB leads at 1080p (337.6 vs.\ 87.6~r/s, $3.85\times$) and 1440p (128.3 vs.\ 49.9~r/s, $2.57\times$), but PostgreSQL overtakes at 4K (23.3 vs.\ 18.1~r/s, $1.28\times$) and widens its lead at 5K (13.6 vs.\ 7.8~r/s, $1.74\times$). Binary retrieval favours MongoDB consistently across \emph{all} resolutions: $2.86\times$ faster at 1080p (2,412 vs.\ 6,906~ms), $2.73\times$ at 1440p (4,260 vs.\ 11,619~ms), $2.49\times$ at 4K (10,143 vs.\ 25,267~ms), and $1.66\times$ at 5K (28,377 vs.\ 47,044~ms). MongoDB's inline \texttt{BinData} storage likely benefits retrieval by keeping payload bytes within the main B-Tree pages, whereas PostgreSQL must follow TOAST pointers to a secondary table; this is consistent with MongoDB's consistent retrieval advantage across all resolutions. Storage amplification shows a resolution-dependent regime change for MongoDB: $0.10\times$ at 1080p (Snappy effectively compresses small documents where JSON metadata dominates) rising to $0.27\times$ at 1440p, then crossing unity to $1.58\times$ at 4K and $3.00\times$ at 5K as the JPEG binary payload dominates and defeats block compression. PostgreSQL maintains stable $1.04\times$ amplification at all resolutions.

\subsection{Carbon Footprint (RQ)}
\label{subsec:carbon_footprint}
Table~\ref{tab:carbon_summary} and Figures~\ref{fig:carbon_footprint}--\ref{fig:carbon_breakdown} report energy and emissions. The complete MongoDB phase consumed 0.0309~kWh over 2,378~s, emitting 20,891~mg CO$_2$eq. PostgreSQL consumed 0.0273~kWh over 2,167~s, emitting 18,439~mg CO$_2$eq---a \textbf{13.3\% carbon advantage for PostgreSQL}. CPU energy accounts for approximately 57\% of total consumption in both phases; MongoDB's CPU energy is 16.1\% higher than PostgreSQL's. The additional carbon cost for MongoDB stems from both longer execution (+9.8\%) and slightly higher average CPU power draw during write-intensive phases.

Per-resolution estimates (Figures~\ref{fig:carbon_footprint} and~\ref{fig:carbon_breakdown}) reveal a nuanced, resolution-dependent picture. At 1080p, MongoDB's much faster insertion ($3.85\times$) means it completes the profile in 41.7~s versus PostgreSQL's 148.7~s, emitting substantially less per resolution (366 vs.\ 1,266~mg CO$_2$eq). The same advantage holds at 1440p (872 vs.\ 2,200~mg). The crossover occurs at 4K, where MongoDB's insertion slows (18.1 vs.\ 23.3~r/s) due to the inherent overhead of serializing and writing 2.99~MB BSON documents inline into B-Tree leaf pages---even though the 9.3~GB MongoDB database at 4K still fits within the 30~GB cache. This causes it to take 8.3\% longer (602 vs.\ 556~s) and emit 11.8\% more (5,289 vs.\ 4,730~mg). At 5K the gap widens significantly: the cumulative database grows to $\sim$29.8~GB, approaching the 30~GB cache limit, so later insert runs trigger buffer pool eviction on top of the large-document serialization overhead. MongoDB takes 47\% longer (1,430 vs.\ 973~s) and emits 52\% more CO$_2$eq (12,562 vs.\ 8,281~mg). The aggregate 13.3\% carbon premium for MongoDB is dominated by the high-resolution profiles, which account for the majority of total benchmark time.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why MongoDB costs more energy.} MongoDB's WiredTiger engine stores entire BSON documents---including multi-megabyte \texttt{BinData} image payloads---inline in B-Tree leaf pages~\cite{mongodb_bson_spec,mongodb_wiredtiger_storage}. At 1080p and 1440p, the accumulated database ($\sim$0.16 and $\sim$0.74~GB respectively) fits entirely within the 30~GB cache, enabling cache-resident writes and explaining MongoDB's strong insertion throughput at these resolutions. The degradation at 4K stems not from cache eviction---the 9.3~GB database still fits comfortably---but from the inherent cost of serializing and writing 2.99~MB documents inline: each insert requires more CPU work per operation and produces less compressible B-Tree page content. At 5K, the cumulative database ($\sim$29.8~GB) approaches the 30~GB cache limit; by the later insert runs the buffer pool has no headroom and eviction threads stall application threads to flush dirty pages~\cite{percona_wiredtiger_eviction}, compounding the large-document overhead. At low resolutions, Snappy compression is effective because structured JSON metadata dominates small documents, explaining MongoDB's $0.10\times$ amplification at 1080p. As payload size grows, the high-entropy JPEG binary defeats block compression~\cite{mongodb_wiredtiger_storage}, causing storage amplification to reach $3.00\times$ at 5K with corresponding extra write I/O energy. GridFS would avoid inline storage but is incompatible with native Time-Series Collections~\cite{mongodb_ts_limitations}.

\textbf{Why PostgreSQL is more efficient.} TOAST~\cite{postgresql_toast_docs} relocates payloads exceeding $\sim$2~KB out-of-line into a sequentially-organised secondary table, leaving only a small pointer in the main hypertable row. This keeps the primary B-Tree compact for fast index traversal regardless of resolution, produces stable $1.04\times$ amplification, and avoids the large-page write overhead observed in MongoDB at higher resolutions. The resulting lower I/O volume contributes to PostgreSQL's shorter total execution time (9.8\% less than MongoDB at the phase level) and lower energy consumption. Notably, MongoDB's inline storage \emph{does} deliver faster binary retrieval at all resolutions ($1.7$--$2.9\times$)---a genuine advantage of the document model for read-heavy workloads---but this benefit is outweighed in energy terms by MongoDB's write amplification at 4K and above.

\textbf{Implications for green building monitoring.} At 1080p MongoDB's inline storage is actually advantageous---faster retrieval, better compression, and lower carbon per profile---so the appropriate choice depends on camera resolution. Above 1440p, PostgreSQL with TimescaleDB is both faster for insertion and more carbon-efficient. The per-row carbon difference at 4K is modest in isolation, but accumulates with ingestion volume. MongoDB paired with an external object store (MinIO, S3) should be evaluated for large-payload scenarios where retrieval speed is paramount.

\textbf{Scale-up indication.} The benchmark measurements provide a per-row emission indicator. At 4K, dividing the measured phase emission rate by the insertion rate yields $\sim$0.49~mg CO$_2$eq per row inserted for MongoDB and $\sim$0.37~mg for PostgreSQL---a $\sim$33\% higher per-row cost. This indicator is derived from a batch benchmark on a single machine and should not be extrapolated directly to production deployments, where workload patterns, concurrency, and hardware differ. It nonetheless illustrates that the relative carbon cost difference per ingested frame grows with resolution and accumulates with data volume.

\textbf{Threats to validity.} Results are from a single host; distributed deployments may differ. Quality-90\% JPEG payloads represent a realistic but not worst-case scenario; lower-quality images would reduce payload size and shift the crossover point. CPU and RAM power are measured via Intel RAPL hardware counters (\texttt{tracking\_mode=machine}); per-resolution emissions are proportional estimates based on a constant power-rate assumption. Both phases are measured under identical hardware conditions, so the relative comparison remains valid.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This study measured the carbon footprint of PostgreSQL (TimescaleDB) and MongoDB for building-monitoring IoT image workloads across 1080p--5K inside Docker containers using CodeCarbon with RAPL-based hardware energy measurement.

\textbf{RQ answer:} MongoDB emits 20,891~mg CO$_2$eq (0.0309~kWh) versus PostgreSQL's 18,439~mg CO$_2$eq (0.0273~kWh)---a \textbf{13.3\% carbon premium for MongoDB} overall. The premium stems from BSON inline serialization overhead at 4K and buffer pool pressure as the 5K working set approaches the 30~GB cache limit, prolonging execution (+9.8\%) and raising CPU energy (+16.1\%). Per-resolution estimates show PostgreSQL emitting \emph{more} at 1080p--1440p (where MongoDB's faster completion dominates) but less at 4K--5K where MongoDB's write amplification takes effect.

These results offer actionable guidance for green IoT deployments. At 1080p and 1440p, MongoDB is faster \emph{and} more carbon-efficient per profile due to faster completion. At 4K and above, PostgreSQL is the better choice under the tested conditions: it is faster for insertion, emits less carbon, and maintains $1.04\times$ storage amplification versus MongoDB's $3.00\times$ at 5K. MongoDB retains a consistent advantage in binary retrieval speed ($1.7$--$2.9\times$ faster) across all resolutions, making it attractive for read-heavy workloads.

Future work will evaluate hybrid architectures that combine PostgreSQL or MongoDB for metadata and time-series indexing with an external binary object store (MinIO, S3) to decouple payload storage from the database engine. Extending the benchmark to 6K resolutions and video payloads would further characterise the crossover dynamics.
