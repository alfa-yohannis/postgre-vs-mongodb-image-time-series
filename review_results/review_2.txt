The authors benchmark PostgreSQL with inline BYTEA storage (PG) against an architecture that externalizes binary payloads to MinIO object storage (PM) across five image resolutions. While the experimental setup is clearly described, the research questions remain limited in scope and do not sufficiently extend beyond expected outcomes. In particular, the observed performance advantage of PM over PG—especially for higher image resolutions—is largely predictable given the well-documented limitations of storing large binary objects within relational databases. As a result, the novelty and contribution of the study are somewhat constrained.
Authors are encouraged to consider expanding the research questions to explore less obvious scenarios, such as transactional guarantees between PG and PM architectures. Also, authors may include additional baselines, such as alternative storage strategies (e.g., PostgreSQL large objects, other object storage systems, or hybrid caching approaches) to provide a more comprehensive comparison.

This paper presents a benchmark comparison of two storage architectures for image-based time-series workloads evaluated across five image resolutions, ranging from 480p to 4K UHD, within containerized environments. The findings indicate that PM is the preferred architecture in terms of both throughput and latency for IoT and CE vision systems processing image payloads across all tested resolutions. In contrast, PG BYTEA remains suitable when single-service simplicity is preferred over performance, as it avoids the consistency and backup coordination overhead of the dual-service PM design.

The paper is well written and well organized, with all key sections properly covered. The methodology and experimental setup are explained clearly, making the study easy to follow. In addition, the discussion section is comprehensive and provides meaningful interpretation of the results rather than merely reporting metrics. However, several aspects could be further improved to strengthen the overall quality of the work:

1. The comparative scope may be somewhat limited by using only PostgreSQL as the relational database baseline. Since the paper focuses on benchmarking storage architectures within the relational database domain, it would strengthen the study to either include additional relational database systems or more clearly justify why PostgreSQL was selected as the representative baseline. It would be valuable to discuss whether similar performance trends are expected with other relational databases, or whether some observations may be specific to PostgreSQL’s implementation. Providing this clarification would improve both the interpretability and generalizability of the findings.

2. Could the authors provide an example of a representative sample row stored during the experiment? This would help readers better understand the structure of the evaluated workload.

3. The reported results are informative about the current workload size. However, it would be valuable to discuss whether the same performance trend (with PM outperforming alternatives) is expected under substantially larger-scale data ingestion scenarios.
