CONSOLIDATED REVIEW FEEDBACK — ALL PAPERS

Paper key:
  Paper 1 = alfa_yohannis_1.tex  (postgre-vs-mongodb-image-time-series)
  Paper 2 = alfa_yohannis_2.tex  (postgre-vs-postgre-minio-image-time-series)
  Paper 3 = alfa_yohannis_3.tex  (postgre-vs-mongodb-carbon-footprint)
  Paper 4 = alfa_yohannis_4.tex  (postgre-vs-postgre-minio-carbon-footprint)

The following actionable critiques are distilled from all four individual
reviews into common themes applicable across the paper set.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. MISSING STORAGE HARDWARE SPECIFICATION
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 1, Paper 3  (Papers 2 and 4 already list the Samsung
               NVMe SSD model.)
   Source: review_3.txt

   The methodology sections describe CPU, RAM, and OS but omit the type and
   model of the storage device (NVMe SSD, SATA SSD, or HDD).  Because every
   paper centres on storage amplification, cache eviction, and I/O bottlenecks,
   readers need this information to judge whether results are I/O-bound or
   compute-bound.
   Action: add storage device make/model/interface to the Environment section.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. WORKLOAD REALISM
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_1.txt

   The benchmarks use sequential single-client insertion and retrieval.
   Reviewers note the absence of:
     • concurrent / multi-client access,
     • mixed read/write workloads,
     • diverse query patterns (range scans, aggregations, playback).
   Action: acknowledge these limitations explicitly in Threats to Validity
   and outline them as future work.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. BATCH SIZE SENSITIVITY
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_3.txt

   All papers use a fixed batch size of 50 records.  Reviewers ask how
   changing the batch size would affect the observed crossover points
   (e.g., the 1440p–4K throughput inversion).
   Action: add a sentence in the methodology or threats section discussing
   whether the batch size was chosen to favour neither engine and noting that
   alternative batch sizes could shift the crossover.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. RUN VARIANCE / JUSTIFICATION FOR 5 RUNS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_1.txt

   Each metric is measured over 5 runs, but no paper reports variance,
   standard deviation, or confidence intervals.  Reviewers ask whether 5 runs
   are sufficient and whether variance is low enough to support the claims.
   Action: report standard deviations or interquartile ranges (boxplots
   already show spread visually — reference them and/or add a brief numeric
   summary in text or tables).

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. FORMATTING AND UNIT CONSISTENCY
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 1, Paper 3  (Papers 2 and 4 already use consistent
               notation.)
   Source: review_3.txt

   a) Multiplier notation: some places write "0.1 times" or "3 times" while
      tables use "0.10×" and "3.00×".  Use a single consistent format
      throughout (prefer the symbolic "0.10×" style).
   b) Time units: body text mixes seconds ("2.5 s") with the millisecond
      values shown in tables ("2542 ms").  Pick one unit per context and
      convert consistently so readers can cross-reference prose and tables
      without mental arithmetic.
   Action: audit every multiplier and time reference for consistency.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. SCOPE JUSTIFICATION AND GENERALISABILITY
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 2, Paper 4  (PG-vs-PM papers where reviewers question
               why only PostgreSQL is the relational baseline.)
   Source: review_2.txt

   Reviewers note that some findings are "largely predictable" given known
   limitations of inline binary storage.  They also ask why only PostgreSQL
   was chosen as the relational baseline (rather than MySQL, MariaDB, etc.).
   Action: add a brief justification for the database selection (e.g.,
   PostgreSQL's dominance in time-series via TimescaleDB) and discuss whether
   similar trends are expected with other relational engines.  Strengthen
   novelty framing around the precise crossover quantification, not just the
   directional finding.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. REPRESENTATIVE DATA EXAMPLE
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 2, Paper 4  (Papers 1 and 3 already include schema
               listings with sample rows/documents.)
   Source: review_2.txt

   Reviewers request a concrete example of a stored row/document so readers
   can visualise the actual workload structure (metadata fields, payload
   size, schema layout).
   Action: add a small illustrative example — a single representative row
   for each architecture — ideally in the methodology section or as a
   listing.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. SCALABILITY DISCUSSION
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_2.txt

   Results are reported for 2,000 rows.  Reviewers ask whether the same
   performance trends hold under substantially larger-scale ingestion
   (e.g., millions of rows, sustained multi-day capture).
   Action: add a paragraph in Discussion addressing expected behaviour at
   larger scales, referencing known scaling properties of TOAST, WiredTiger
   cache, and object-store semantics.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. ADDITIONAL REFERENCES
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_3.txt

   Reviewers suggest citing more conference and journal papers that address
   database performance metrics for binary or large-payload workloads to
   strengthen the related work grounding.
   Action: survey and add 2–4 additional relevant references.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. RAW DATA SIZE BASELINE FOR STORAGE AMPLIFICATION
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 1, Paper 3  (Papers 2 and 4 already provide enough
               context in their storage tables.)
   Source: review_3.txt

   Storage amplification is reported as a ratio but the absolute raw
   (logical) data size is not stated explicitly for every resolution.
   Reviewers ask, e.g., "for 1920×1080 at ~1.09 MB, what is the total raw
   size for 2,000 rows?"
   Action: add a Total Raw Data Size column or footnote so readers can
   independently verify the amplification ratio.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
11. CROSS-PAPER SIMILARITY (IEEE COMPLIANCE)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers, but especially Paper 4 (flagged for similarity
               with Paper 1's Introduction and Related Work).  Papers 1 & 3
               and Papers 2 & 4 share substantial verbatim overlap.
   Source: review_4.txt

   The Introduction and Related Work sections across the four papers share
   substantial verbatim overlap.  One reviewer explicitly flags a single-
   source similarity above the 10 % IEEE threshold.
   Action: rewrite Introduction and Related Work for each paper so that
   shared background is paraphrased differently and each paper foregrounds
   its own unique contribution from the opening paragraphs.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
12. CONFIGURATION FAIRNESS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 1, Paper 3  (the two PG-vs-MongoDB papers where
               WiredTiger cache sizing is a factor.)
   Source: review_1.txt

   For the PG-vs-MongoDB papers: MongoDB's WiredTiger cache is set to a
   specific value (e.g., 2 GB or 30 GB).  Reviewers ask whether this is
   fair relative to PostgreSQL's OS-level caching and whether tuning could
   delay the observed failures.
   Action: justify the chosen cache configuration, clarify PostgreSQL's
   equivalent memory path, and note in Threats to Validity that larger cache
   could delay — but not eliminate — the architectural limitations.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
13. INCLUDE ALL TESTED RESOLUTIONS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: Paper 1  (mentions testing 360p, 480p, 720p but excludes
               them "for brevity".)
   Source: review_3.txt

   Some papers mention that lower resolutions (360p, 480p, 720p) were tested
   but excluded "for brevity".  Reviewers argue that including them — even
   in a compact supplementary table — would reinforce the trend narrative.
   Action: add a summary table or appendix row for all tested resolutions.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
14. FUTURE WORK DIRECTIONS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: review_4.txt, review_2.txt

   Reviewers suggest several extensions that should be mentioned:
     • distributed / sharded / multi-node deployments,
     • PostgreSQL Large Objects (LO) as an alternative to BYTEA,
     • hybrid metadata-in-DB + payload-in-object-store strategies,
     • GPU-accelerated downstream analytics.
   Action: ensure the future-work paragraph covers at least distributed
   deployments and PostgreSQL LO where relevant.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
15. PAGES AND ABSTRACT LIMIT
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
   Applies to: All papers (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, Paper 4)
   Source: conference formatting guidelines

   Full papers must not exceed 6 pages. Each manuscript must include an
   abstract of approximately 150 words and provide a complete description
   of the research, including objectives, methods, results, and conclusions.
